The active voice verb expansion rule is

T (M) (have + -en) (be + -ing) MV

T = tense

Tense is a grammatical concept; it may not be the same as "time." English has two tenses: present and past. The tense affects the first item in the verb phrase. If the tense is present, the first item in the verb phrase will be present tense. If the tense is past, the first item in the verb phrase will be past. Tense is always a part of every active voice verb phrase.

(M) = modal auxiliary verb

The M is in parentheses because it isn¹t always present. A modal verb indicates probability, possibility, obligation, and the like. English has six major modal verbs. Four of them have present and past tense forms:

PresentPast
WillWould
ShallShould
CanCould
MayMight

"Tense" refers to the grammatical form of the verbs, not to "time." If you "may eat," that doesn¹t mean you are eating now, in the present. And if you "might eat," that doesn¹t mean you were eating in the past. In fact, we often use modal verbs to convey information about the future, as in "You will eat."

Two more modals have no past tense form:

  • Must
  • Ought to

(Other groups of words, called "quasi-modals", also sometimes fill this slot, e.g., "have to.")

(have + -en) = A form of the verb "have" followed by the ­en form of whatever verb comes next

These items are in parentheses because they aren¹t always present. When a "have" is present, the verb that immediately follows it MUST take the ­en form. The (have + -en) conveys the perfect aspect, which includes information such as verb completion, duration, and repetition‹time-related elements that are not related to past, present, or future. The ­en form of the verb will only end in the letters ­en some of the time; with regular verbs, the ­en form looks the same as the ­ed form. To figure out the ­en form of a verb, use it in a sentence with "have," e.g., "I have ___________." Here are some examples of the different forms:

Base -en Form -ed Form
Think Thought Thought
Run Run Ran
Go Gone Went
Take Taken Took
Move Moved Moved
Beat Beaten Beat
Yawn Yawned Yawned
Smile Smiled Smiled
Eat Eaten Ate

(be + -ing) = A form of the verb "be" followed by the ­ing form of whatever verb comes next

These items are in parentheses because they aren¹t always present. When a "be" is present, the verb that immediately follows it MUST take the ­ing form. The (be + -ing) conveys the progressive aspect, which includes information such as verb completion, duration, and repetition‹time-related elements that are not related to past, present, or future.

MV = Main verb. The main verb is not in parentheses because every verb phrase must contain a main verb

The main verb is always the last item in the verb phrase. The main verb is the verb that decides that pattern of the sentence.
